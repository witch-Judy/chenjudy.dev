> Any feature that can be solved by an API is not a moat.

This is the deepest realization I've had while building AI products recently.

We used to think building AI products required a "technical moat": proprietary models, unique algorithms, being faster or better than competitors on some engineering dimension. That was the logic of the past decade of mobile internet startups.

But that logic is breaking down.

When the foundational capabilities of GPT, Claude, and Gemini leap forward every few months, when developers worldwide are calling the same set of APIs, any feature that can be replicated with "general intelligence + public data" is essentially built on rented ground. When the flood comes, these are the first to wash away.

So where does the real moat for AI-native products actually lie?

---

## 1. Two Paths of Doing Science

I'm increasingly convinced that AI entrepreneurship and scientific research are fundamentally the same thing: studying the deep patterns of a domain and transforming them into reusable systems.

Scientists study patterns, write papers, and let peers replicate them. AI founders study patterns, write them into code, and let every user interaction validate and iterate on those patterns. The only difference: one publishes in journals, the other ships on the App Store.

---

## 2. What Is ILLUCIO Researching?

Our product is called ILLUCIO. On the surface, it's an AI reading companion that helps people understand complex texts.

But at its core, we're running a scientific experiment on **"how humans understand and learn information."** We focus on extremely granular cognitive struggles:

- When someone gets stuck on a dense text, where exactly are they stuck?
- Is it unfamiliar terminology, a logical leap, or a gap in background knowledge?
- What kind of "scaffolding" intervention creates an instant Aha Moment, rather than just dragging a progress bar to the end?

Cognitive psychology has theories, but theories are a long way from "actually working in a real product." We need real user data to feed the theories, adjust intervention strategies, and validate the feedback loop.

**The lab is the product. Every click is an experiment.**

---

## 3. Two Moats, Both Essential

This perspective clarifies the survival line for AI products. There are two real moats:

- **Data moat (private mineral deposit):** Behavioral traces others can't access. Where users experience cognitive overload, what forms of explanation actually worked — only you have this data.
- **Scientific moat (proprietary formula):** Patterns distilled from the data. Effective heuristics for specific types of thinking impasses, scaffolding strategies for different learner stages — these insights only come from deep experimentation.

Data without science is sitting on a gold mine and begging. Many companies own massive datasets but have never distilled patterns from them — the data just sleeps on hard drives. Science without data is armchair speculation. You may have profound theories, but without real-world feeding, theory can never land.

**Only when both form a closed loop do you have a real moat.**

---

## 4. Why This Realization Cures Anxiety

Many AI founders are anxious: if the model updates tomorrow, will my product die?

If your moat is just "I wrapped a feature around an API," then that anxiety is inevitable. But if your moat is **"I have a scientific understanding of a cognitive domain that nobody else has"**, then you have resilience.

Technology gets commoditized, open-sourced, and leapfrogged by the next foundation model. But patterns are hard to replicate. Patterns take time to accumulate, data to refine, and countless rounds of validation and failure.

**In the purely digital world, technical moats are dying. Cognitive moats are rising.**

Now, when I see the news that "another foundation model just got updated," I no longer feel the anxiety of being chased. Instead, I feel excited: great, my "lab equipment" just got upgraded — this research can start from an even higher baseline.

That feeling? It's a good one.
